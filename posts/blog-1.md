### Part 1: Project Background and Brief Understanding

Based on the student blog template provided by the course, I understood that the A1/A3 Dev Blog was not asking me to build a complicated new website. Instead, it helped me learn how to organise content, adjust visual style, and publish the project process through GitHub Pages. Combined with the introduction to web applications, I started to realise that this course is not only about page presentation, but also about building a workflow from design, to development, to deployment.

### Part 2: Early Concept Comparison and Selection

![](assets/images/1.1.png)

At the early stage, our group proposed five directions: a healthy meal sharing platform, an ADHD support community, a business-user communication platform, a first-week settlement task platform, and a medical help platform. We used a Concept Comparison Matrix to compare them through criteria such as specific user problem, BlaBla brief fit, task-oriented structure, community value, database feasibility, MVP scope, and team confidence.

In the end, we chose the Medical Help Task Platform. Compared with the First-Week Settlement Platform, it had a smaller scope, a clearer pain point, and could be broken down into more specific task steps. The settlement platform also had potential, but its scope was too broad and could make the database and MVP difficult to control.

### Part 3: User Research and Problem Analysis

![](assets/images/1.2.png)

Through online ethnography and questionnaire research, we found that students’ problem was not only a lack of information. The bigger issue was that medical information was scattered, difficult to judge, and hard to turn into specific actions. Many Chinese international students rely on Xiaohongshu, WeChat groups, or friends’ experiences, but these sources are fragmented, hard to save long-term, and not always reliable.

![](assets/images/1.3.png)

We focused our target community on Chinese international students who have just arrived in Sydney and are unfamiliar with the local healthcare system. Their main pain points include scattered information, decision-making difficulty, difficulty saving useful experiences, lack of local knowledge, and low confidence in taking action. Based on this, we narrowed the project into a structured “See a doctor in Sydney” task platform. Through a step-by-step pathway, place cards, and peer experiences, the platform aims to help students complete the doctor-seeking process with more confidence.

### Part 4: Turning Problems into Design Opportunities and Early Risks

![](assets/images/1.4.png)

We turned user pain points into design opportunities: scattered information became a structured task pathway; unclear steps became step-by-step guidance; unclear locations became related place cards; and difficult-to-save peer experiences became peer experience cards. Week 4 and Week 5 also helped me realise that if user experiences, places, and questions need to be reused long-term, we need to think early about what information should be saved, read, or updated.

We also identified early risks, including medical information accuracy, emergency safety, location updates, privacy protection, the applicability of peer experiences, and MVP scope control.

### Design Decisions

1. Through the Concept Comparison Matrix, we evaluated five project ideas and selected the more focused and controllable Medical Help Task Platform.

2. Based on research and user pain point analysis, we narrowed the project into a structured “See a doctor in Sydney” task platform. We used step-by-step pathways, related place cards, and peer experience cards to support users in judging, preparing, and taking action.

### Personal Reflection

In this process, I learned that design is not about choosing the idea that looks the biggest. It is about making trade-offs between user needs, evidence, and feasibility. At first, we had many ideas, but after connecting them with the idea of a task-oriented web application, I realised that a platform with too broad a scope could easily become a general information collection. Through this process, I better understood why narrowing the project direction early is important.
